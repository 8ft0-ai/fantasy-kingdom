# Simulation, Chronicle and cinematic direction

ANNALS is not only a rendered map. Its central product idea is that the kingdom's history should be legible while it is happening.

Simulation systems update material state: population, stores, trade, loyalty, characters, dynasties, wars, disasters and long-run recovery. Meaningful outcomes are converted into structured events before presentation. Those event records provide a stable location, participants, consequences, priority and deterministic identity.

The Chronicle and cinematic director consume the same underlying event. The Chronicle turns it into annalistic prose; the director turns its location and priority into a camera opportunity. This shared source prevents the text from describing one event while the camera follows an unrelated approximation.

Historical memory extends the model beyond a stream of isolated messages. Significant events can receive stable names and relationships, leave physical scars, and be recalled by later events that share a place, person, house or consequence. The Chronicle can therefore refer back to a specific prior event rather than merely repeating recent prose.

The simulation is implemented through layered wrappers rather than one monolithic update function. Its effective order keeps dependencies intelligible: time and weather advance, base settlement state changes, named lives and dynasties react, economy and trade move, memories and scars update, wars and threats resolve, stability governors apply, and presentation-worthy outcomes reach the Chronicle.

The director is intentionally selective. Priority, shot grammar and category cooldowns prevent every event from becoming an interruption. Manual camera input temporarily suspends direction, while Watch mode gives the director greater control so the realm can be observed as a story.

This architecture makes cause and effect visible without turning the Chronicle into a log dump or the camera into random motion.
